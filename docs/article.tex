\begin{abstract}
Predictive maintenance and robust control are twin pillars of modern industrial reliability. This paper presents a hybrid framework that synergizes data-driven prognostics with control-theoretic optimization. \color{red} Acá sugiero indicar el gap de literatura, por ejemplo, Recently, several large deep learning models have been proposed to address this task. However, such models typically require substantial effort for hyperparameter optimization and training, and often impose significant computational and capacity constraints at inference time. These limitations can be prohibitive in control-oriented architectures or resource-constrained decision-making settings.  To advance in a different direction, \color{black} we propose a lightweight Artificial Neural Network (ANN) for predicting the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of turbofan engines, utilizing the NASA C-MAPSS dataset. Unlike recent heavy deep learning architectures, our approach employs Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) to dynamically tune the ANN architecture, balancing accuracy with computational efficiency. Furthermore, the evolutionary optimization framework is extended to the control domain, where a Fractional-Order PID (FOPID) controller is tuned via PSO to ensure stability in a simulated load-frequency system. The methodology allows for accurate degradation monitoring—validated across four complexity levels (FD001--FD004)—and demonstrates how prognostic data can inform robust control actions. The findings suggest that, for prognostic-driven decision-making and control applications, architectural optimization and system integration may be more critical than increasing representational depth.
\end{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Remaining Useful Life prediction, NASA C-MAPSS, particle swarm optimization, artificial neural networks, mixed-integer linear programming, model pruning

\section{Introduction}

The paradigm shift toward Industry~4.0 has significantly increased the relevance of Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) as a core component of intelligent industrial systems. Accurate estimation of the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of critical assets—such as aircraft engines—enables a transition from reactive and preventive maintenance toward predictive strategies, reducing unplanned downtime, maintenance costs, and the risk of catastrophic failures \cite{Saxena2008}. In parallel, modern industrial plants operate under progressively harsher and more uncertain conditions, where system degradation and parametric drift challenge conventional control strategies. These trends motivate the need for integrated approaches that jointly address \emph{measurement} (health assessment) and \emph{control} (robust closed-loop behavior).

The NASA Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS) dataset has emerged as a \textcolor{blue}{widely studied benchmark \cite{Saxena2008}} for RUL prediction research. Early contributions relied on physics-based and statistical models; however, recent advances have been dominated by data-driven methods. \textcolor{blue}{Lei et al.~\cite{Lei2018Machinery} extensively documented this transition toward machine learning formalisms designed to trace complex degradation paths.} In particular, recurrent neural networks have demonstrated strong capabilities for modeling temporal degradation. \textcolor{blue}{Wu et al.~\cite{Wu2018Remaining} established the efficacy of long short-term memory (LSTM) architectures over standard feed-forward networks, an observation structurally consistent with} Peringal et al.~\cite{Peringal2024} \textcolor{blue}{who noted} that LSTM networks outperform classical Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) by explicitly capturing sequential dependencies. Subsequent studies have further increased architectural complexity: Elsherif et al.~\cite{Elsherif2025} proposed a Convolutional Autoencoder combined with an attention-based LSTM (CAELSTM) to learn spatial–temporal features, while Chen~\cite{Chen2024} introduced a Transformer-based architecture with position-sensitive attention to handle long-term dependencies\textcolor{blue}{, parallel to findings by Mo et al.~\cite{Mo2021Remaining}}.

\textcolor{blue}{As network depth increases, achieving optimal performance mathematically necessitates robust hyperparameter optimization (HPO). A concise examination of HPO methodologies for neural networks illustrates a structured progression \cite{Yang2020Hyperparameter}. Grid Search combined with Cross-Validation offers exhaustive parameter grid evaluation but scales poorly against high dimensionality. Alternatively, Random Search coupled with Cross-Validation reliably samples independent network parameters, frequently isolating effective topologies under tighter computational constraints. To navigate highly parameterized continuous spaces systematically, Bayesian Optimization employs surrogate probabilistic models to intelligently navigate parameter spaces without exhaustive iterations, an approach formalized by Snoek et al.~\cite{Snoek2012Practical}. In the prognostics domain, Xue et al.~\cite{Xue2025} recently implemented Bayesian approaches to structure multi-modal Transformer–LSTM models, effectively automating architecture selection while operating with substantial computational complexity.}

\textcolor{blue}{These trajectories expose a critical bipartite gap: the tension between pursuing maximum predictive accuracy through increasing network depth and the requisite computational tractability for deployment. The exponential scaling of deep architectures, such as massive Transformers and CAELSTM models, incurs substantial environmental costs in terms of energy consumption and carbon footprint, giving rise to the imperative of ``Green AI'' \cite{Schwartz2020GreenAI, Strubell2019Energy, Garcia2019Estimation, Dhar2021Survey, Wu2022Sustainable, Patterson2021Carbon}. Heavy networks require specialized hardware, meaning that their deployment implicitly scales up physical, real-world resource consumption—including significant electrical power and water for data-center cooling. Conversely, advancing intelligent sensing requires Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) that are lightweight enough to be embedded locally on edge devices or small micro-controllers without relying on centralized, large-scale computing \cite{Li2023Edge, Merenda2020Edge, Wang2020Convergence, Zhou2019Edge, Jiang2021Model, Chen2019Deep}. Furthermore, while extensive research focuses on refining RUL predictions in isolation, there remains a notable absence of unified evolutionary frameworks that link measurement insights directly to robust control tuning.}

\textcolor{blue}{To bridge this gap, this paper introduces a holistic architecture that couples a highly optimized, lightweight prognostic model with a robust control mechanism, actively prioritizing computational efficiency and environmental sustainability.} Building on this premise, a single Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) engine is employed to address two tightly related tasks: (i) automated architecture selection for a \textcolor{blue}{computationally tractable and environmentally viable} MLP-based RUL predictor, and (ii) parameter tuning of a Fractional-Order PID (FOPID) controller. \textcolor{blue}{Instead of escalating network depth, we demonstrate that a precisely tuned, shallow ANN operating on explicit temporal embeddings is capable of capturing essential degradation trajectories while remaining small enough for practical edge deployment.} Inspired by the work of Gong et al.~\cite{Gong2023}, the FOPID controller introduces additional degrees of freedom through fractional integration and differentiation orders, enhancing robustness under nonlinear and degraded operating conditions. By coupling optimized health estimation with optimized feedback control, the proposed framework advances a holistic interpretation of \textit{Measurement and Control}, in which prognostic information is not an endpoint but a functional input to closed-loop system design.

\section{Discussion}

The experiments support two conclusions that are easy to miss if only one metric is considered. On the one hand, the pruning stage is not meaningless: under the current 10\% keep-ratio budget, the selected pruned models consistently improve internal validation MSE, which indicates that the pruning-plus-fine-tuning stage acts as a strong structural regularizer on the held-out development engines. On the other hand, the official benchmark test set reveals that this regularization is too aggressive at the present compression level. In all four subsets, the dense reference remains the better final predictor once the official NASA split is consulted.

The topology table adds another important diagnostic. All four selected final architectures are two-hidden-layer networks, and the reported pruning method for each subset is a magnitude-based fallback after attempting the reduced-neighborhood exact MILP. This outcome is methodologically informative rather than merely inconvenient. It shows that the reduced-neighborhood exact formulation meaningfully shrinks the binary search space, but still struggles to deliver a feasible incumbent fast enough for the present 30-second limit and the current layer widths. In other words, the mathematical pruning model is useful as a guide for structured compression, yet exact sparse-structure optimization remains a computational bottleneck at this scale.

The subset pattern is also consistent with the descriptive statistics in the data card. FD001 and FD003 are single-regime datasets with more homogeneous operating settings, and they remain easier for lightweight MLPs. FD002 and FD004 require generalization across multiple operating regimes, and their higher official-test errors confirm that regime diversity is a stronger challenge for this family of models than simply adding a second fault mode. This makes the current workflow especially valuable as a measurement study: it separates the effects of data complexity, model size, and optimization strategy without relying on test-set reuse.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper reframed the project as a measurement-only RUL study and evaluated a reproducible ANN workflow built around low-cost PSO screening, MILP-guided pruning, and selective retuning on the NASA C-MAPSS benchmark. The main methodological contribution is not a claim of global optimality, but a disciplined separation of roles: PSO screens structures cheaply, pruning compresses shortlisted models on a calibration subset, and final evaluation remains tied to a single use of the official NASA test split.

Under the current aggressive pruning budget, the pruned candidates retain roughly one tenth of the active arcs and improve validation error, but the dense reference still provides the best official-test accuracy across FD001--FD004. The results therefore motivate two immediate next steps: less aggressive keep ratios and stronger exact-pruning neighborhoods for two-hidden-layer models. Even with that limitation, the workflow provides a scientifically defensible baseline for studying how lightweight architecture search and structured pruning interact in turbofan RUL prediction.

\section{Ideas}

\subsection{MILP to prone NNs}
\textbf{Sets and indices}
\begin{align}
\mathcal{N} &= \{1,\dots,N\} \quad \text{training samples}, \\
\mathcal{H} &= \{1,\dots,H\} \quad \text{hidden neurons}, \\
\mathcal{I} &= \{1,\dots,d\} \quad \text{input features}, \\
\mathcal{O} &= \{1,\dots,m\} \quad \text{output neurons}.
\end{align}

\textbf{Given data}
\begin{align}
x^n_i &:\ \text{input feature } i \text{ of sample } n, \\
\hat{y}^n_k &:\ \text{output of the original (reference) neural network for sample } n \text{ and output } k.
\end{align}
Let $W^{(1)}_{ji},\; b^{(1)}_j$ be the weights and biases from input to hidden layer, and $W^{(2)}_{kj},\; b^{(2)}_k$ the weights and biases from hidden to output layer of the pruned network before arc deletion. We also define big-\(M\) constants for the ReLU formulation:
\[
L^n_j \le a^n_j \le U^n_j,\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
where \(a^n_j\) is the pre-activation of hidden neuron \(j\) for sample \(n\), and \(L^n_j,U^n_j\) are valid lower and upper bounds.

\textbf{Decision variables}
\[
z^{(1)}_{ji}\in\{0,1\} \qquad \forall j\in\mathcal{H},\; i\in\mathcal{I},
\]
\[
z^{(2)}_{kj}\in\{0,1\} \qquad \forall k\in\mathcal{O},\; j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
where \(z^{(1)}_{ji}=1\) if arc \((i,j)\) is kept, and \(z^{(2)}_{kj}=1\) if arc \((j,k)\) is kept.

For each sample \(n\):
\[
a^n_j \in \mathbb{R} \qquad \forall j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
h^n_j \ge 0 \qquad \forall j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
\delta^n_j \in \{0,1\} \qquad \forall j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
y^n_k \in \mathbb{R} \qquad \forall k\in\mathcal{O}.
\]

\[
\textbf{Objective}
\]
Minimize the quadratic error between the pruned network and the reference network:
\[
\min \sum_{n\in\mathcal{N}} \sum_{k\in\mathcal{O}}
\left(y^n_k-\hat{y}^n_k\right)^2.
\]

\[
\textbf{Layer equations}
\]

Hidden-layer pre-activation:
\[
a^n_j = \sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}} W^{(1)}_{ji}\, x^n_i \, z^{(1)}_{ji} + b^{(1)}_j,
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H}.
\]

ReLU activation \(h^n_j=\max\{0,a^n_j\}\), linearized with big-\(M\):
\[
h^n_j \ge a^n_j,
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
h^n_j \ge 0,
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
h^n_j \le a^n_j - L^n_j(1-\delta^n_j),
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},
\]
\[
h^n_j \le U^n_j \delta^n_j,
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H}.
\]

Output layer:
\[
y^n_k = \sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}} W^{(2)}_{kj}\, h^n_j \, z^{(2)}_{kj} + b^{(2)}_k,
\qquad \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; k\in\mathcal{O}.
\]

\[
\textbf{Pruning budget}
\]

Let the total number of arcs in the original two-layer network be
\[
A_{\text{tot}} = |\mathcal{H}||\mathcal{I}| + |\mathcal{O}||\mathcal{H}|.
\]
If at least an \(\alpha\) fraction of arcs must be deleted, then the number of kept arcs can be at most \((1-\alpha)A_{\text{tot}}\):
\[
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}} z^{(1)}_{ji}
+
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{O}}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}} z^{(2)}_{kj}
\;\le\;
(1-\alpha)\, A_{\text{tot}}.
\]

Equivalently, if exactly an \(\alpha\) fraction of arcs must be deleted, impose:
\[
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}} z^{(1)}_{ji}
+
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{O}}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}} z^{(2)}_{kj}
\;=\;
(1-\alpha)\, A_{\text{tot}}.
\]

\[
\textbf{Domain constraints}
\]
\[
z^{(1)}_{ji}\in\{0,1\},\qquad
z^{(2)}_{kj}\in\{0,1\},\qquad
\delta^n_j\in\{0,1\},
\]
\[
a^n_j\in\mathbb{R},\qquad
h^n_j\ge 0,\qquad
y^n_k\in\mathbb{R}.
\]

\[
\textbf{Complete model}
\]
\[
\begin{aligned}
\min \quad & \sum_{n\in\mathcal{N}} \sum_{k\in\mathcal{O}}
\left(y^n_k-\hat{y}^n_k\right)^2 \\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& a^n_j = \sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}} W^{(1)}_{ji}\, x^n_i \, z^{(1)}_{ji} + b^{(1)}_j,
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& h^n_j \ge a^n_j,
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& h^n_j \ge 0,
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& h^n_j \le a^n_j - L^n_j(1-\delta^n_j),
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& h^n_j \le U^n_j \delta^n_j,
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& y^n_k = \sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}} W^{(2)}_{kj}\, h^n_j \, z^{(2)}_{kj} + b^{(2)}_k,
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; k\in\mathcal{O},\\
& \sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}} z^{(1)}_{ji}
+
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{O}}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{H}} z^{(2)}_{kj}
\le (1-\alpha)\,A_{\text{tot}}, \\
& z^{(1)}_{ji}\in\{0,1\},
&& \forall j\in\mathcal{H},\; i\in\mathcal{I},\\
& z^{(2)}_{kj}\in\{0,1\},
&& \forall k\in\mathcal{O},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& \delta^n_j\in\{0,1\},
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\\
& a^n_j\in\mathbb{R},\; h^n_j\ge 0,\; y^n_k\in\mathbb{R},
&& \forall n\in\mathcal{N},\; j\in\mathcal{H},\; k\in\mathcal{O}.
\end{aligned}
\]
