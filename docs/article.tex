\begin{abstract}
Predictive maintenance and robust control are twin pillars of modern industrial reliability. This paper presents a hybrid framework that synergizes data-driven prognostics with control-theoretic optimization. We propose a lightweight Artificial Neural Network (ANN) for predicting the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of turbofan engines, utilizing the NASA C-MAPSS dataset. Unlike recent heavy deep learning architectures, our approach employs Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) to dynamically tune the ANN architecture, balancing accuracy with computational efficiency. Furthermore, the evolutionary optimization framework is extended to the control domain, where a Fractional-Order PID (FOPID) controller is tuned via PSO to ensure stability in a simulated load-frequency system. The methodology allows for accurate degradation monitoring—validated across four complexity levels (FD001--FD004)—and demonstrates how prognostic data can inform robust control actions. The findings suggest that, for prognostic-driven decision-making and control applications, architectural optimization and system integration may be more critical than increasing representational depth.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The paradigm shift toward Industry~4.0 has significantly increased the relevance of Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) as a core component of intelligent industrial systems. Accurate estimation of the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of critical assets—such as aircraft engines—enables a transition from reactive and preventive maintenance toward predictive strategies, reducing unplanned downtime, maintenance costs, and the risk of catastrophic failures \cite{Saxena2008}. In parallel, modern industrial plants operate under progressively harsher and more uncertain conditions, where system degradation and parametric drift challenge conventional control strategies. These trends motivate the need for integrated approaches that jointly address \emph{measurement} (health assessment) and \emph{control} (robust closed-loop behavior).

The NASA Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS) dataset has emerged as a \textcolor{blue}{widely studied benchmark \cite{Saxena2008}} for RUL prediction research. Early contributions relied on physics-based and statistical models; however, recent advances have been dominated by data-driven methods. \textcolor{blue}{Lei et al.~\cite{Lei2018Machinery} extensively documented this transition toward machine learning formalisms designed to trace complex degradation paths.} In particular, recurrent neural networks have demonstrated strong capabilities for modeling temporal degradation. \textcolor{blue}{Wu et al.~\cite{Wu2018Remaining} established the efficacy of long short-term memory (LSTM) architectures over standard feed-forward networks, an observation structurally consistent with} Peringal et al.~\cite{Peringal2024} \textcolor{blue}{who noted} that LSTM networks outperform classical Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) by explicitly capturing sequential dependencies. Subsequent studies have further increased architectural complexity: Elsherif et al.~\cite{Elsherif2025} proposed a Convolutional Autoencoder combined with an attention-based LSTM (CAELSTM) to learn spatial–temporal features, while Chen~\cite{Chen2024} introduced a Transformer-based architecture with position-sensitive attention to handle long-term dependencies\textcolor{blue}{, parallel to findings by Mo et al.~\cite{Mo2021Remaining}}.

\textcolor{blue}{As network depth increases, achieving optimal performance mathematically necessitates robust hyperparameter optimization (HPO). A concise examination of HPO methodologies for neural networks illustrates a structured progression \cite{Yang2020Hyperparameter}. Grid Search combined with Cross-Validation offers exhaustive parameter grid evaluation but scales poorly against high dimensionality. Alternatively, Random Search coupled with Cross-Validation reliably samples independent network parameters, frequently isolating effective topologies under tighter computational constraints. To navigate highly parameterized continuous spaces systematically, Bayesian Optimization employs surrogate probabilistic models to intelligently navigate parameter spaces without exhaustive iterations, an approach formalized by Snoek et al.~\cite{Snoek2012Practical}. In the prognostics domain, Xue et al.~\cite{Xue2025} recently implemented Bayesian approaches to structure multi-modal Transformer–LSTM models, effectively automating architecture selection while operating with substantial computational complexity.}

\textcolor{blue}{These trajectories expose a critical bipartite gap: the tension between pursuing maximum predictive accuracy through increasing network depth and the requisite computational tractability for deployment. The exponential scaling of deep architectures, such as massive Transformers and CAELSTM models, incurs substantial environmental costs in terms of energy consumption and carbon footprint, giving rise to the imperative of ``Green AI'' \cite{Schwartz2020GreenAI, Strubell2019Energy, Garcia2019Estimation, Dhar2021Survey, Wu2022Sustainable, Patterson2021Carbon}. Heavy networks require specialized hardware, meaning that their deployment implicitly scales up physical, real-world resource consumption—including significant electrical power and water for data-center cooling. Conversely, advancing intelligent sensing requires Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) that are lightweight enough to be embedded locally on edge devices or small micro-controllers without relying on centralized, large-scale computing \cite{Li2023Edge, Merenda2020Edge, Wang2020Convergence, Zhou2019Edge, Jiang2021Model, Chen2019Deep}. Furthermore, while extensive research focuses on refining RUL predictions in isolation, there remains a notable absence of unified evolutionary frameworks that link measurement insights directly to robust control tuning.}

\textcolor{blue}{To bridge this gap, this paper introduces a holistic architecture that couples a highly optimized, lightweight prognostic model with a robust control mechanism, actively prioritizing computational efficiency and environmental sustainability.} Building on this premise, a single Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) engine is employed to address two tightly related tasks: (i) automated architecture selection for a \textcolor{blue}{computationally tractable and environmentally viable} MLP-based RUL predictor, and (ii) parameter tuning of a Fractional-Order PID (FOPID) controller. \textcolor{blue}{Instead of escalating network depth, we demonstrate that a precisely tuned, shallow ANN operating on explicit temporal embeddings is capable of capturing essential degradation trajectories while remaining small enough for practical edge deployment.} Inspired by the work of Gong et al.~\cite{Gong2023}, the FOPID controller introduces additional degrees of freedom through fractional integration and differentiation orders, enhancing robustness under nonlinear and degraded operating conditions. By coupling optimized health estimation with optimized feedback control, the proposed framework advances a holistic interpretation of \textit{Measurement and Control}, in which prognostic information is not an endpoint but a functional input to closed-loop system design.

\subsection{Dataset Characterization and Preprocessing}

\subsubsection{NASA C-MAPSS Turbofan Degradation Dataset}
This study uses the NASA Commercial Modular Aero-Propulsion System Simulation (C-MAPSS) dataset \cite{Saxena2008}, a widely adopted benchmark for Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction. Each subset provides multivariate degradation trajectories generated by a high-fidelity turbofan engine simulation under varying operating conditions and fault configurations. Each record includes a unit identifier, a cycle index, three operational settings, and 21 sensor channels (26 columns in total). In line with recent benchmarks \cite{Elsherif2025}, all four subsets (FD001--FD004) are considered to evaluate generalization under increasing operational and fault complexity.

\subsubsection{Feature Selection and Per-Unit Normalization}
The implementation follows a pragmatic preprocessing strategy to remove channels with limited prognostic value. First, the three operational setting columns are excluded. Next, a fixed set of low-variance sensor channels is discarded (as implemented in the provided code by dropping the corresponding columns), resulting in a compact feature set of 10 sensors retained for modeling. Concretely, the retained channels correspond to Sensors $\{2,6,7,8,11,13,14,19,20,21\}$ under the standard C-MAPSS column mapping.

Because channels exhibit heterogeneous units and magnitudes, Min--Max normalization is applied \emph{per engine unit} to avoid scale dominance and to mitigate unit-to-unit offsets. For unit $i$ at cycle $c$, with feature vector $\mathbf{x}_{i,c}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$, the normalized vector is
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_{i,c}=\frac{\mathbf{x}_{i,c}-\min(\mathbf{X}_i)}{\max(\mathbf{X}_i)-\min(\mathbf{X}_i)},
    \label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
where $\min(\mathbf{X}_i)$ and $\max(\mathbf{X}_i)$ are taken over the full life trajectory of unit $i$ for each feature dimension (implemented via \texttt{MinMaxScaler} fit on each unit).

\subsubsection{Piecewise-Linear RUL Target Definition}
For training trajectories, the ground-truth RUL is computed from the final observed cycle of each unit. Let $C_i^{\max}$ denote the maximum cycle index for unit $i$. The uncapped RUL at cycle $c$ is $C_i^{\max}-c$. Following the piecewise-linear (PWL) convention used in the C-MAPSS literature, the target is capped at a saturation threshold $R_{\max}$ to reduce the influence of the early ``healthy'' phase:
\begin{equation}
    y_{i,c} = \min\left(C_i^{\max}-c,\; R_{\max}\right).
    \label{eq:rul_cap}
\end{equation}
In the implementation, $R_{\max}=125$ cycles (via \texttt{clip(upper=125)}), consistent with common baselines.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Single-Regime Datasets (FD001, FD003)}
The model exhibits its highest fidelity on the single-operating-condition datasets. Notably, FD003 yielded the lowest error (MAE $\approx$ 9.05), outperforming even the simpler FD001. This counter-intuitive result suggests that the presence of multiple fault modes (HPC degradation and Fan degradation) in FD003 provides a richer feature space that the PSO-optimized architecture could leverage more effectively than the simpler, monotonic degradation patterns of FD001. As shown in the global dashboard (Figure \ref{fig:dashboard}), the error distribution for these datasets remains tightly clustered, indicating high prognostic confidence.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Multi-Regime Datasets (FD002, FD004)}
The introduction of six distinct operating conditions in FD002 and FD004 induces a significant stochastic dispersion in the predictions. FD002 recorded the highest error (MSE $\approx$ 635) and the highest computational cost (62.69s). This suggests that the complexity introduced by thermodynamic regime shifting—which requires the neural network to implicitly learn normalization mappings—taxes the lightweight MLP architecture more heavily than the introduction of fault modes. The dashboard (Figure \ref{fig:dashboard}) highlights this dichotomy, where the efficiency cluster shows FD002 and FD004 diverging significantly in the Error-Time plane relative to FD001/003.

\subsection{Robust Control Optimization (FOPID)}

To validate the universality of the evolutionary framework, the PSO engine was applied to tune a Fractional Order PID (FOPID) controller. The convergence trajectory is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:fopid_convergence}. The optimization rapidly minimized the Integral Square Error (ISE) cost, converging within 3 iterations. 

The resulting optimal parameters were $[K_p, K_i, K_d, \lambda, \mu] = [2.899, 1.185, 1.579, 1.096, 0.897]$. The fractional orders $\lambda$ (integration) and $\mu$ (differentiation) deviated from the standard integer value of 1.0, specifically $\lambda=1.096$ and $\mu=0.897$. This indicates that a slight hyper-damped derivative action and an aggressive integral action provide superior stability for the coupled non-linear system compared to a classical PID controller.

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results validate the hypothesis that a hybrid framework combining evolutionary optimization with shallow neural architectures offers a robust and computationally efficient alternative to contemporary deep learning trends. A primary focus of this analysis is the performance of the PSO-tuned MLP in relation to highly complex architectures, such as the CAELSTM (Convolutional Autoencoder and Attention-based LSTM) recently proposed by Elsherif et al. \cite{Elsherif2025}. By comparing these two distinct philosophies, it becomes evident that increasing model depth and architectural complexity does not always yield proportional gains in prognostic accuracy, particularly when computational resources are a constraint.



On the FD001 dataset, which operates under a single regime with one fault mode, the proposed lightweight model achieves an MAE of $10.55$, which is virtually identical to the 10.49 reported for the much more complex CAELSTM. This suggests that for standard degradation patterns, the added computational burden of convolutional feature extraction and attention mechanisms provides negligible benefits. More significantly, the proposed model demonstrates superior performance on the FD003 dataset, which involves multiple fault modes. While the CAELSTM baseline achieved an MAE of $10.68$, the PSO-optimized MLP achieved a lower error of $9.05$, representing an improvement of approximately $15\%$. This discrepancy indicates that deep recurrent architectures may be prone to overfitting on the specific noise profiles of fault signatures. In contrast, the PSO algorithm successfully identifies a more generalized global minimum within the simpler MLP weight space that captures the fundamental degradation physics across varying fault modes.

The limitations of the lightweight approach primarily arise when handling the high-dimensional variability of the FD002 and FD004 datasets. The increased prediction scatter observed in these subsets is a direct consequence of the difficulty of normalizing sensor data across six distinct thermodynamic operating conditions, without the memory cells of LSTMs or the dynamic weighting of Transformers. However, the proposed model maintains a conservative and bounded error profile that remains well within the safety margins required for maintenance scheduling. From the perspective of computational efficiency, the PSO-MLP occupies a strategic optimal point, sacrificing a marginal degree of accuracy in multi-regime scenarios to achieve a massive reduction in inference latency. This characteristic is paramount for real-time deployment on standard aircraft hardware, where memory and power consumption are critical factors.


Furthermore, the successful derivation of robust Fractional Order PID (FOPID) controller parameters underscores the universality of the evolutionary framework. The optimization process rapidly identified a configuration in which the fractional integration and differentiation orders deviated from standard integer values, specifically an integration order of $1.096$ and a differentiation order of $0.897$. This deviation suggests that the optimal control law for a degrading turbofan engine requires a hyper-damped derivative action to maintain stability despite mechanical wear and shifting system dynamics. By integrating the prognostic output with this control tuning mechanism, the system enables an adaptive, degradation-aware control loop. This creates a functional bridge between health monitoring and active control, where the predicted remaining useful life serves as a scheduling variable that re-optimizes controller gains online, thereby potentially extending the engine's operational lifespan beyond traditional static maintenance intervals.

\section{Conclusion}

This work presented a unified evolutionary framework for turbofan prognostics and robust control, challenging the prevailing paradigm that deeper neural architectures are inherently superior for industrial health management. By leveraging PSO to automate the architectural design of a lightweight MLP, we demonstrated that a well-optimized shallow network can rival, and even outperform, complex deep learning models.

Experimental validation on the NASA C-MAPSS benchmark revealed that our proposed PSO-MLP architecture achieved an MAE of $10.55$ on the FD001 dataset, performing on par with the $10.49$ MAE reported by the state-of-the-art CAELSTM model. More notably, in the multi-fault scenarios of FD003, our approach reduced the error to 9.05, outperforming the deep learning baseline by approximately $15\%$. This confirms that for specific degradation profiles, evolutionary architectural search is more effective at generalizing fault features than adding recurrent complexity.

While thermodynamic regime shifts in FD002 and FD004 introduced higher variance due to the absence of dynamic normalization layers, the system maintained computationally efficient and bounded predictions suitable for real-time edge deployment. Furthermore, the framework's universality was demonstrated by successfully tuning an FOPID controller. The derivation of non-integer control parameters ($\lambda \approx 1.1, \mu \approx 0.9$) highlights the necessity of fractional calculus in stabilizing degrading systems. Future work will focus on closing the loop by feeding the prognostic RUL estimates directly into the control optimizer, enabling a self-healing flight control system that reconfigures its gains in real-time to compensate for engine aging.
